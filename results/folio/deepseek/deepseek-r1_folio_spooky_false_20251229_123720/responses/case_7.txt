1. Monkeypox virus occurrence may lead to Monkeypox (expressed as a possibility via `MonkeypoxVirusOccursIn` implying potential `GetMonkeypox`).
2. Monkeypox virus occurs in some animals (`∃ b, Animal b ∧ MonkeypoxVirusOccursIn b`).
3. Humans are mammals (`∀ b, Human b → Mammal b`).
4. Mammals are animals (`∀ b, Mammal b → Animal b`).
5. Monkeypox causes tiredness (`∀ b, GetMonkeypox b → FeelTired b`).
6. Flu causes tiredness in humans (`∀ b, Human b → GetFlu b → FeelTired b`).

The statement "No one gets the flu" is formalized as `∀ b, ¬ GetFlu b`. Its negation (`∃ b, GetFlu b`) cannot be proven because:
- Premise 2 confirms virus occurrence in some animal, but not necessarily in humans.
- Premise 1 uses "may" (non-guaranteed), so virus occurrence doesn't ensure Monkeypox.
- Tiredness symptoms (Premises 5-6) have multiple possible causes (Monkeypox or flu), but no premise forces tiredness to occur or links it exclusively to flu.
- No premise asserts that any being actually contracts flu.

Thus, the proof of `∃ b, GetFlu b` fails, as shown by the `sorry` in the theorem.
