The premises state that all squares are four-sided and all four-sided things are shapes. The conclusion is that all squares are shapes.

In Lean, we model this as:
- `obj` as a type representing objects.
- Predicates `Square`, `FourSided`, and `Shape` on `obj`.
- Axiom `A1`: if something is a square, then it is four-sided.
- Axiom `A2`: if something is four-sided, then it is a shape.

The conclusion is `∀ x, Square x → Shape x`. To attempt proving the negation of the conclusion (i.e., `∃ x, Square x ∧ ¬Shape x`):
1. We assume `∃ x, Square x ∧ ¬Shape x`.
2. However, for any such `x`, `A1` implies `Square x → FourSided x`, and `A2` implies `FourSided x → Shape x`, leading to a contradiction (since `x` would be both `Shape x` and `¬Shape x`).
3. This contradiction proves that the negation of the conclusion is false, meaning the original conclusion must be true.

Since we cannot prove the negation of the conclusion without encountering a contradiction, the attempt to prove the statement false fails.
