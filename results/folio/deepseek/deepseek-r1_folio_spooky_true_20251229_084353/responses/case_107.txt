The premises lead to a contradiction in one case but do not provide sufficient information to prove the conclusion in the other case. Specifically:
- Premise 1: Spilling food implies not notably tidy
- Premise 3: Cleanly implies notably tidy
- Peter's disjunction: Either (spills food and cleanly) or (not spills food and not cleanly)

The first case (spills food and cleanly) leads to a contradiction because:
- Cleanly implies notably tidy (Premise 3)
- Spills food implies not notably tidy (Premise 1)
- Thus, notably tidy and not notably tidy cannot both hold

Therefore, Peter must be in the second case (not spills food and not cleanly). However:
- Not cleanly does not imply not notably tidy (Premise 3 is one-directional)
- Not spills food does not imply notably tidy (Premise 1 only gives the contrapositive: notably tidy implies not spills food)
- No other premises connect to Peter's tidiness

Thus, "Peter is notably tidy" cannot be proven from the given premises.
