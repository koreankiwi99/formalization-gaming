The reasoning involves translating the given premises into logical axioms and proving the conclusion about Tom. The key steps are:

1. Tom is a pet owner (premise 5).
2. All pet owners are animal lovers (premise 4), so Tom is an animal lover.
3. All animal lovers are nice to animals (premise 3), so Tom is nice to animals.
4. No one nice to animals is mean to animals (premise 1), so Tom cannot be mean to animals.
5. Therefore, Tom cannot be both grumpy and mean to animals (since he isn't mean to animals).

This proof holds regardless of whether Tom is grumpy or not, as the absence of meanness to animals suffices to negate the conjunction.

<lean>
axiom obj : Type
axiom Tom : obj
axiom NiceToAnimals : obj → Prop
axiom MeanToAnimals : obj → Prop
axiom Grumpy : obj → Prop
axiom AnimalLover : obj → Prop
axiom PetOwner : obj → Prop

-- Premises
axiom P1 : ∀ x, NiceToAnimals x → ¬ MeanToAnimals x
axiom P2 : ∃ x, Grumpy x ∧ MeanToAnimals x
axiom P3 : ∀ x, AnimalLover x → NiceToAnimals x
axiom P4 : ∀ x, PetOwner x → AnimalLover x
axiom P5 : PetOwner Tom

-- Conclusion: Tom is not both grumpy and mean to animals
theorem tom_not_grumpy_and_mean : ¬(Grumpy Tom ∧ MeanToAnimals Tom) := by
  -- Show Tom is nice to animals and thus not mean to animals
  have h_lover : AnimalLover Tom := P4 Tom P5
  have h_nice : NiceToAnimals Tom := P3 Tom h_lover
  have h_not_mean : ¬ MeanToAnimals Tom := P1 Tom h_nice
  -- If Tom were both grumpy and mean, it would contradict h_not_mean
  intro h
  exact h_not_mean h.2
