The reasoning involves translating the context and the statement into logical propositions and checking provability using Lean.

**Lean Analysis:**
<lean>
variable (LikesMusic: Prop)   -- George likes music
variable (HasAccess: Prop)     -- George has access to a program
variable (WantsToCompose: Prop)
variable (CanCompose: Prop)
variable (WillCompose: Prop)

axiom a1 : LikesMusic → WantsToCompose
axiom a2 : HasAccess → CanCompose
axiom a3 : CanCompose ∧ WantsToCompose → WillCompose

-- The query: ¬WillCompose → ¬CanCompose
-- We cannot prove this from the axioms, nor can we prove its negation.
</lean>

**Explanation:**
- **Axiom 1:** If George likes music, he wants to compose (`LikesMusic → WantsToCompose`).
- **Axiom 2:** If he has access to a program, he can compose (`HasAccess → CanCompose`).
- **Axiom 3:** If he can compose and wants to compose, he will compose (`CanCompose ∧ WantsToCompose → WillCompose`).

The statement to evaluate is:  
"If George will not compose, then George cannot compose" (`¬WillCompose → ¬CanCompose`).

**Why this is uncertain:**
1. **The statement cannot be proven:**  
   Suppose ¬WillCompose holds (George does not compose). By Axiom 3, this implies ¬(CanCompose ∧ WantsToCompose), which is equivalent to ¬CanCompose ∨ ¬WantsToCompose. However:
   - This does not force ¬CanCompose to be true, since the disjunction might hold due to ¬WantsToCompose (e.g., George can compose but lacks desire).
   - Without knowing George's access to the program (`HasAccess`) or his musical interest (`LikesMusic`), we cannot rule out that he is able to compose (`CanCompose`).

2. **The negation cannot be proven:**  
   The negation of the statement is ¬WillCompose ∧ CanCompose (he does not compose *despite* being able to). This scenario could hold if:
   - George has access (so `CanCompose` by Axiom 2),
   - George does not like music (so we cannot force `WantsToCompose` via Axiom 1),
   - Hence, he lacks desire to compose (`¬WantsToCompose`), preventing composition by Axiom 3.

Since the axioms alone do not force a contradiction with either the statement or its negation, the statement remains **Uncertain**.

ANSWER: Uncertain
