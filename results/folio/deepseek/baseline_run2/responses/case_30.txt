The Lean code models the given context and checks if "Djokovic is a Grand Slam champion" is provable.

- `person` is the type of individuals.
- `mixer` indicates attendance at the mixer.
- Predicates like `GrandSlamChampion` represent the properties.
- Axioms correspond to the premises:
  - A1: Everyone at the mixer is a Grand Slam champion or an Oscar-nominated actor.
  - A2-Every Oscar-nominated actor at the mixer is a celebrity.
  - A3, A4, A5, A6: Chain of implications for different groups.
  - A7: Well-paid people live in tax havens (irrelevant to the goal).
  - `djokovic_cond`: The specific condition about Djokovic.
  - `djokovic_at_mixer`: Djokovic attends the mixer.

The goal `GrandSlamChampion Djokovic` is:
- **Not provable**: Djokovic could be an Oscar-nominated actor (by A1), leading to `Celebrity Djokovic` and `WellPaid Djokovic` but not necessarily to `GrandSlamChampion Djokovic`.
- **Refutation not provable**: Djokovic could be a Grand Slam champion (by A1), leading to `ProfessionalTennisPlayer Djokovic`, `Athlete Djokovic`, `Famous Djokovic`, and potentially `WellPaid Djokovic` via `djokovic_cond`, with no contradiction.
- The condition about Djokovic (`Famous Djokovic ∧ Athlete Djokovic → WellPaid Djokovic`) holds vacuously if he is not both famous and an athlete (e.g., if an Oscar-nominated actor not in sports).

Thus, neither affirmation nor refutation is derivable.
