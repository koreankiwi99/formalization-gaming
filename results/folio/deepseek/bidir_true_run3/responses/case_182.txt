The reasoning is based on the given context and the statement to evaluate. The key steps are:

1. From "All pet owners love animals" and "Tom is a pet owner", we conclude Tom is an animal lover.
2. From "All animal lovers are nice to animals", Tom being an animal lover implies he is nice to animals.
3. From "No one nice to animals is also mean to animals", Tom being nice to animals implies he is not mean to animals.
4. Therefore, Tom cannot be both grumpy and mean to animals (since he is not mean to animals at all).

The existence of grumpy people who are mean to animals (second premise) does not affect Tom's case, as his pet ownership requires him not to be mean to animals.

<lean>
axiom obj : Type
axiom Tom : obj
axiom Grumpy : obj → Prop
axiom MeanToAnimals : obj → Prop
axiom NiceToAnimals : obj → Prop
axiom AnimalLover : obj → Prop
axiom PetOwner : obj → Prop

axiom A1 : ∀ (x : obj), NiceToAnimals x → ¬ MeanToAnimals x
axiom A2 : ∃ (x : obj), Grumpy x ∧ MeanToAnimals x
axiom A3 : ∀ (x : obj), AnimalLover x → NiceToAnimals x
axiom A4 : ∀ (x : obj), PetOwner x → AnimalLover x
axiom A5 : PetOwner Tom

theorem goal : ¬ (Grumpy Tom ∧ MeanToAnimals Tom) := by
  have T_lover : AnimalLover Tom := A4 Tom A5
  have T_nice : NiceToAnimals Tom := A3 Tom T_lover
  have T_not_mean : ¬ MeanToAnimals Tom := A1 Tom T_nice
  intro H
  exact T_not_mean H.right
</lean>

ANSWER: True
