The reasoning involves evaluating the statement: "If Rockie is cute and calm, then Rockie is a skittish turtle" based on the given premises. 

Premises:
1. All spotted rabbits are cute.
2. Some spotted turtles exist (but this doesn't directly affect Rockie).
3. Only rabbits and squirrels are spotted.
4. Skittish implies not calm.
5. All spotted squirrels are skittish.
6. Rockie is spotted and calm.

Analysis:
- From premise 6, Rockie is spotted and calm.
- Premise 3 implies Rockie is either a rabbit or a squirrel.
- If Rockie were a squirrel (case 1):
  - Premise 5 would make Rockie skittish.
  - Premise 4 would then imply Rockie is not calm, contradicting premise 6.
  - This case is impossible.
- Thus, Rockie must be a rabbit (case 2):
  - Premise 1 implies Rockie is cute (since spotted and rabbit).
  - Premise 6 confirms Rockie is calm.
  - The antecedent "Rockie is cute and calm" is true.
  - However, the consequent requires Rockie to be a skittish turtle:
    - Rockie is a rabbit, not a turtle (premise 3 excludes turtles from spotted animals).
    - Rockie is calm, so cannot be skittish (premise 4).
  - The consequent is false.

The implication fails because the antecedent is true while the consequent is false. Therefore, the statement cannot be proven from the premises.

theorem target : (Cute Rockie ∧ Calm Rockie) → (Skittish Rockie ∧ Turtle Rockie) :=
