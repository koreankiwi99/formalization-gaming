<lean>
axiom Person : Type
axiom Bonnie : Person

axiom PerformsOften : Person → Prop
axiom AttendsEngaged : Person → Prop
axiom InactiveDisinterested : Person → Prop
axiom Chaperones : Person → Prop
axiom IsStudent : Person → Prop
axiom YoungChildOrTeen : Person → Prop
axiom WishFurther : Person → Prop

axiom Premise1 : ∀ p : Person, PerformsOften p → AttendsEngaged p
axiom Premise2 : ∀ p : Person, PerformsOften p ∨ InactiveDisinterested p
axiom Premise3 : ∀ p : Person, Chaperones p → ¬ IsStudent p
axiom Premise4 : ∀ p : Person, InactiveDisinterested p → Chaperones p
axiom Premise5 : ∀ p : Person, YoungChildOrTeen p ∧ WishFurther p → IsStudent p

-- Derived from Bonnie's specific premise: 
-- "Bonnie either both attends and is engaged and is a student OR is not (attends and engaged and not student)"
-- This simplifies to: AttendsEngaged Bonnie → IsStudent Bonnie
axiom Bonnie_fact : AttendsEngaged Bonnie → IsStudent Bonnie

-- Attempt to prove that Bonnie does NOT perform often
theorem not_performs : ¬ PerformsOften Bonnie :=
  by
  -- Assume Bonnie performs often
  intro hPerform
  -- From Premise1, Bonnie attends and is engaged
  have hEngaged := Premise1 Bonnie hPerform
  -- From Bonnie_fact, Bonnie must be a student
  have hStudent := Bonnie_fact hEngaged
  -- From Premise2, Bonnie either performs often or is inactive/disinterested
  -- We already have performs often, so consider inactive case
  have hCases := Premise2 Bonnie
  cases hCases with
  | inl _ => 
      -- Case: Bonnie performs often - no immediate contradiction
      -- We need to show Bonnie cannot be inactive simultaneously
      have hNotInactive : ¬ InactiveDisinterested Bonnie := by
        intro hInactive
        have hChaperones := Premise4 Bonnie hInactive
        have hNotStudent := Premise3 Bonnie hChaperones
        contradiction  -- hStudent and hNotStudent conflict
      -- But no contradiction from performing often alone
      -- Premise5 cannot be applied without knowing Bonnie's age/wishes
      sorry  -- Cannot derive contradiction here
  | inr hInactive => 
      -- Case: Bonnie is inactive - but we assumed she performs often (hPerform)
      -- If she is inactive, she cannot perform often (contradiction path)
      have hChaperones := Premise4 Bonnie hInactive
      have hNotStudent := Premise3 Bonnie hChaperones
      -- From inactive: ¬IsStudent, but from performing often: IsStudent
      contradiction  -- This branch leads to contradiction
  -- However, the first branch (performs often) remains unrefuted without additional information
  sorry  -- Overall proof fails

-- Since we cannot fully prove ¬PerformsOften Bonnie, we indicate failure
</lean>

ANSWER: Failure

The Lean code was structured to attempt a proof of the negation of "Bonnie performs in school talent shows often." However, the proof could not be completed due to insufficient information to derive a contradiction. Specifically:

1. Assuming Bonnie performs often leads to:
   - Bonnie being engaged (Premise1)
   - Bonnie being a student (Bonnie_fact)

2. Premise2 forces a disjunction:
   - Case 1 (performs often): No contradiction emerges since we lack information about Bonnie's age/educational wishes (Premise5) or other properties that would force inconsistency.
   - Case 2 (inactive): Leads to contradiction (chaperoning implies ¬student), but this branch is already refuted when assuming she performs often.

The key gap: While inactivity would contradict the student status derived from performing often, Premise2's inclusive OR doesn't prohibit Bonnie from being active and performing often without additional constraints. Premise5 cannot be applied without knowing Bonnie is a young child/teenager who wishes to further her education.

Thus, the negation cannot be proven.
