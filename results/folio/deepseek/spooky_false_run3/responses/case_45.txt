The premises state that all cats are mammals and that there are some pets that are not mammals. The conclusion claims that no pets are cats. To disprove this conclusion, one would need to find at least one pet that is also a cat. However, the given premises do not provide evidence of any cat, nor do they guarantee that any cat must be a pet. While there exists a pet that is not a mammal (and thus cannot be a cat), there is no information about other pets or any existing cats. Therefore, the possibility remains that the conclusion could be true in scenarios where cats do not exist or are not kept as pets. Since no contradiction can be derived from the premises and the negation of the conclusion cannot be proven, the proof fails.

<lean>
axiom obj : Type
axiom Cat : obj → Prop
axiom Mammal : obj → Prop
axiom Pet : obj → Prop

-- Premises
axiom all_cats_are_mammals : ∀ x, Cat x → Mammal x
axiom some_pets_nonmammal : ∃ x, Pet x ∧ ¬ Mammal x

-- Negation of the conclusion "No pets are cats" is "Some pet is a cat"
theorem not_conclusion : ∃ x, Pet x ∧ Cat x := by
  -- We have a witness from the second premise: some pet that is not a mammal
  rcases some_pets_nonmammal with ⟨w, hPet, hNotMammal⟩
  -- This specific pet cannot be a cat because if it were, it would be a mammal (contradiction)
  have : ¬ Cat w := fun hCat => hNotMammal (all_cats_are_mammals w hCat)
  -- No evidence exists for a pet that is also a cat. The premises do not
  -- force any cat to exist or require pets beyond the known non-mammal.
  -- Hence, the goal cannot be proven.
  sorry  -- Proof incomplete; no evidence for a pet-cat
</lean>
