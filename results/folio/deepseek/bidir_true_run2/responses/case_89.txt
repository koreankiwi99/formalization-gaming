The context mentions that Harry read "Walden" and, by the given rules, reading a book causes one to gain knowledge and gaining knowledge makes one smarter. Therefore, Harry is a specific example of a smarter person who has gained knowledge. This proves that there exists at least one smarter person who has gained knowledge. However, the statement "A smarter person has gained knowledge" is ambiguous: it could mean either "every smarter person has gained knowledge" (universal) or "there exists a smarter person who has gained knowledge" (existential).

- **Universal interpretation (∀ p, becomes_smarter p → gains_knowledge p)**: This is unprovable. The axioms only state that gaining knowledge leads to becoming smarter (**A3**), not vice versa. Other ways to become smarter without gaining knowledge might exist.
- **Existential interpretation (∃ p, becomes_smarter p ∧ gains_knowledge p)**: This is provable. Harry gains knowledge by reading "Walden" (from **A2** applied to **T1**) and becomes smarter from gaining knowledge (from **A3**).

Given that Harry serves as proof for the existential interpretation, and natural language often allows "a" to imply existence in such contexts, we prove the existential interpretation.

<lean>
axiom Person : Type
axiom Book : Type
axiom Walden : Book
axiom Harry : Person

axiom gains_knowledge : Person → Prop
axiom becomes_smarter : Person → Prop
axiom reads : Person → Book → Prop

axiom A2 : ∀ (p : Person) (b : Book), reads p b → gains_knowledge p
axiom A3 : ∀ p : Person, gains_knowledge p → becomes_smarter p
axiom T1 : reads Harry Walden

theorem exists_smarter_person : ∃ p, becomes_smarter p ∧ gains_knowledge p :=
  ⟨Harry, ⟨A3 Harry (A2 Harry Walden T1), A2 Harry Walden T1⟩⟩
</lean>

ANSWER: True
