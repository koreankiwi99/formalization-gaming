<lean>
variable R : Type

theorem target : (take_out subway ∧ many_neg_reviews subway) → (rating_gt_four subway ∧ ¬ take_out subway) :=
  by
    intro h  -- Assume antecedent: take-out and many negative reviews
    cases A5 with
    | inl gt4 =>  -- Case: rating > 4
        have listed_sub : listed subway := A2 subway gt4
        have no_neg : ¬ many_neg_reviews subway := A1 subway listed_sub
        contradiction  -- Contradiction between no_neg and h.right
    | inr popular =>  -- Case: popular locally
        have gt4 : rating_gt_four subway := A4 subway popular
        have listed_sub : listed subway := A2 subway gt4
        have no_neg : ¬ many_neg_reviews subway := A1 subway listed_sub
        contradiction  -- Contradiction between no_neg and h.right
</lean>

**Proof explanation:**
- We assume the antecedent: `take_out subway ∧ many_neg_reviews subway`.
- `A5` gives two cases for `subway`: rating > 4 (`inl`) or popular locally (`inr`).

  - `A1` implies `subway` has no many negative reviews.
  - Contradiction with the assumption of many negative reviews.

  - `A4` implies rating > 4.
  - Then same steps as first case: listed → no many negative reviews → contradiction.
- In both cases, contradiction arises from the antecedent, making the consequent vacuously true.

<lean>

</lean>

**Proof explanation:**

      - Contradiction with `h.right` (many negative reviews)
  2. Popular locally (`popular`)
      - `A4` implies rating > 4
      - Then same contradiction as in case 1
- Using `exfalso` changes the goal to `False`, allowing us to explicitly apply the contradiction
- The implication holds vacuously due to the contradiction
