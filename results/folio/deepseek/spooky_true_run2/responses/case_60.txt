The reasoning text describes a scenario involving international students and their visa types (F1 or J1), with specific conditions for working in the US. Mike is given as an international student who must apply for CPT if he wants to work. The goal is to prove that Mike has an F1 visa.

The premises are:
1. All international students have either an F1 or J1 visa.
2. If an international student has an F1 visa and wants to work, they must apply for CPT or OPT.
3. Mike is an international student.
4. If Mike wants to work, he must apply for CPT.

The conclusion is that Mike has an F1 visa.

Analysis:
- From premise 1 and 3, Mike has either F1 or J1.
- Premise 2 applies only to F1 students. For J1 students, no information is provided about work requirements.
- Premise 4 states Mike's work requirement (apply for CPT) but does not specify how this relates to his visa type.
- The information about Mike applying for CPT (from premise 4) is consistent with both visa types:
  - If Mike has F1, applying for CPT satisfies premise 2 (since CPT is one option of CPT/OPT).
  - If Mike has J1, premise 4 could hold independently (since no rules about J1 work requirements are given).
- There is no premise that indicates that applying for CPT implies F1 status, or that J1 students have different work requirements. Thus, evidence from Mike's work application does not force his visa type.

Therefore, the conclusion (Mike has F1) cannot be proven from the given premises.
