The premises state that "A hawk never swims" and "Some birds are hawks." The conclusion is "All birds swim." To evaluate this, I formalized the context in Lean:

- Defined a type `obj` for objects.
- Predicates: `Hawk`, `Bird`, `Swim`.
- Axiom `T1`: Hawks do not swim (∀ x, Hawk x → ¬ Swim x).
- Axiom `T2`: There exists a bird that is a hawk (∃ x, Bird x ∧ Hawk x).

The theorem to prove is `∀ x, Bird x → Swim x`. During the proof process:
- Introduced an arbitrary `x` such that `Bird x`.
- Used `T2` to get a witness `w` with `Bird w` and `Hawk w`.
- By `T1`, `w` does not swim (`¬Swim w`).
- However, there’s no logical link between the arbitrary `x` and the specific `w`: 
  - We cannot confirm that `x = w` (no equality information).
  - We have no premise that all birds are hawks.
- Therefore, we cannot conclude `Swim x` for this arbitrary `x`.

The proof fails due to a counterexample: the witness `w` is a bird that does not swim. This refutes the universal claim that all birds swim. Since the premises imply the negation of the conclusion, the latter is unprovable.
