The natural language reasoning states that all humans are mortal and all Greeks are humans, and the question is whether the statement "Some Greeks are mortal" is false. In logical terms, the premises are:
- ∀x, Human(x) → Mortal(x)
- ∀x, Greek(x) → Human(x)
The conclusion to evaluate is ∃x, Greek(x) ∧ Mortal(x). The goal is to try to prove the negation of this conclusion, ¬∃x, Greek(x) ∧ Mortal(x).

Using the premises, we can derive ∀x, Greek(x) → Mortal(x) by transitivity (from Greek to Human, and Human to Mortal). The negation of the conclusion is equivalent to ∀x, Greek(x) → ¬Mortal(x). However, from the premises, for any object, if it is Greek, it must be Mortal. This creates a conflict only if there exists a Greek object: if Greek(x) holds, then we have Mortal(x) and the negation requires ¬Mortal(x), leading to a contradiction.

But the premises alone do not guarantee the existence of any Greek object. Without knowing if any Greek exists, the domain could be empty, making the negation true (since there are no Greeks). However, it could also be that a Greek exists (who is Mortal), making the conclusion true and thus the negation false. Therefore, the premises are consistent with both scenarios: one where the negation holds (empty or no Greeks) and one where it does not (when Greeks exist). Consequently, we **cannot prove** the negation of the conclusion from the given premises.
