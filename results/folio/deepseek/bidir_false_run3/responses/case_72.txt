The goal is to prove that the statement "If KO is a growth stock or bought to earn profits from rapid price appreciation, then KO is neither a stock nor is its price volatile" is false. The Lean code models the problem with axioms reflecting the premises:

1. All growth stocks are bought for rapid appreciation.
2. Stocks bought for rapid appreciation are not suitable for retirement funds.
3. Some stocks are growth stocks.
4. All mature stocks are suitable for retirement funds.
5. KO is a mature stock.

The analysis reveals:
- The antecedent (KO is a growth stock or bought for rapid appreciation) is false because:
  - KO is a mature stock (A5) and thus suitable for retirement (A4).
  - If KO were a growth stock (or bought for rapid appreciation), it would not be suitable for retirement (A1 and A2), leading to a contradiction.
- Since the antecedent is false, the entire implication is true (vacuously).
- The consequent (KO is neither a stock nor volatile in price) cannot be evaluated directly, but its falsity is irrelevant because the antecedent is false.

No contradiction arises from assuming the negation of the conclusion. The negation of the implication is `(antecedent) ∧ ¬(consequent)`, which simplifies to `(False) ∧ ...` and is unprovable. Therefore, we cannot prove the original statement false.
