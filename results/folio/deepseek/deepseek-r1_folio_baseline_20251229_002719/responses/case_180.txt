The context states:
- Susan flies to LGA airport, so Susan arrives at LGA.
- For any flight, the departure and arrival airports must be different.
- John flies from LGA airport, so John departs from LGA.

The question is whether John flies to LGA airport (i.e., arrives at LGA). By the rule that departure and arrival airports must differ, if John departs from LGA, he cannot arrive at LGA. Therefore, the statement "John flies to LGA airport" is false.

Lean proof:
- We define types for `Airport` and `Person`.
- Introduce Susan and John as persons, and LGA as an airport.
- Define predicates for departure (`departs`) and arrival (`arrives`).
- Add axioms: Susan arrives at LGA, John departs from LGA, and the flight rule ensures departure and arrival are always different.
- The theorem `John_does_not_arrive_at_LGA` proves that John does not arrive at LGA by showing that if he did, it would violate the flight rule (LGA ≠ LGA leads to contradiction).

<lean>
axiom Airport : Type
axiom Person : Type
axiom Susan : Person
axiom John : Person
axiom LGA : Airport
axiom departs : Person → Airport → Prop
axiom arrives : Person → Airport → Prop
axiom Susan_arrives : arrives Susan LGA
axiom John_departs : departs John LGA
axiom flight_rule : ∀ (p : Person) (d a : Airport), departs p d → arrives p a → d ≠ a

theorem John_does_not_arrive_at_LGA : ¬ arrives John LGA := by
  intro h
  have : LGA ≠ LGA := flight_rule John LGA LGA John_departs h
  exact this rfl
</lean>

ANSWER: False
