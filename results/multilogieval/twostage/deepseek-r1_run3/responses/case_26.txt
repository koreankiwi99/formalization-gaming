=== REASONING ===
We are given two premises:
  Premise1: If a team loses an important game, then the players feel disappointed.
  Premise2: If a team loses a big match, then the players are demoralized.

We are to prove:
  For any team t, if the players are not disappointed and not demoralized, then the team did not lose an important game and did not lose a big match.

In logical terms:
  ∀ (t : Team), ¬ Disappointed t ∧ ¬ Demoralized t → ¬ LosesImportant t ∧ ¬ LosesBig t

We can prove this by assuming we have a team `t` and the hypothesis `h : ¬ Disappointed t ∧ ¬ Demoralized t`.
We need to show two things: 
  1. ¬ LosesImportant t
  2. ¬ LosesBig t

To show ¬ LosesImportant t, we assume `h_imp : LosesImportant t` and derive a contradiction.
From Premise1 and `h_imp`, we get `Disappointed t`. But our hypothesis `h` includes `¬ Disappointed t`, which is a contradiction.

Similarly, for ¬ LosesBig t: assume `h_big : LosesBig t`, then by Premise2 we get `Demoralized t`, which contradicts `¬ Demoralized t` from `h`.

We can use Lean tactics to prove this interactively.

Here is the complete code:


=== RESPONSE ===
<lean>
axiom Team : Type
axiom LosesImportant : Team → Prop
axiom LosesBig : Team → Prop
axiom Disappointed : Team → Prop
axiom Demoralized : Team → Prop

axiom Premise1 : ∀ (t : Team), LosesImportant t → Disappointed t
axiom Premise2 : ∀ (t : Team), LosesBig t → Demoralized t

theorem conclusion : ∀ (t : Team), 
  ¬ Disappointed t ∧ ¬ Demoralized t → 
  ¬ LosesImportant t ∧ ¬ LosesBig t := 
fun t h =>
  have not_disappointed := h.left
  have not_demoralized := h.right
  ⟨
    fun h_imp => not_disappointed (Premise1 t h_imp),
    fun h_big => not_demoralized (Premise2 t h_big)
  ⟩
</lean>
