- `alarm_went_off`: The alarm went off.
- `time_to_get_ready`: It is time to get ready.
- `miss_bus`: John misses the bus.
- `late_for_school`: John is late for school.
- `got_up_late`: John got up late.
- `went_to_bed_late`: John went to bed late.

Premises:
1. If the alarm went off, then it is time to get ready: `alarm_went_off → time_to_get_ready`
2. If John misses the bus, then he is late for school: `miss_bus → late_for_school`
3. Either the alarm went off, or John was not late for school: `alarm_went_off ∨ ¬late_for_school`
4. If John got up late, then he misses the bus: `got_up_late → miss_bus`
5. If John went to bed late, then he got up late: `went_to_bed_late → got_up_late`

Additional given: John knows it is not time to get ready, so `¬time_to_get_ready`.

The goal is to prove that John did **not** go to bed late (`¬went_to_bed_late`).

Proof sketch:
- From `¬time_to_get_ready` and `alarm_went_off → time_to_get_ready`, we derive `¬alarm_went_off`.
- Using `alarm_went_off ∨ ¬late_for_school` and `¬alarm_went_off`, we get `¬late_for_school`.
- From `¬late_for_school` and `miss_bus → late_for_school`, we derive `¬miss_bus`.
- From `¬miss_bus` and `got_up_late → miss_bus`, we derive `¬got_up_late`.
- From `¬got_up_late` and `went_to_bed_late → got_up_late`, we derive `¬went_to_bed_late`.

This proves the negation of the conclusion.
