The context states a general rule: If a student attends lectures regularly, then if they study diligently, they will excel in the course. We are given that Priya studied diligently but did not excel. We need to prove whether Priya attended lectures regularly.

The goal is to prove that Priya did not attend lectures regularly (i.e., ¬AttendsRegularly Priya) from the premises. The reasoning is as follows:
- Assume Priya attended lectures regularly (AttendsRegularly Priya).
- By the general rule, this implies that if she studies diligently, she excels (StudiesDiligently Priya → ExcelsInCourse Priya).
- Since she studied diligently (Diligent), this would force her to excel (ExcelsInCourse Priya).
- But we know she did not excel (NotExcel), leading to a contradiction.
- Therefore, the assumption (AttendsRegularly Priya) must be false.

This proof is formalized in Lean below.

<lean>
axiom Person : Type
axiom Priya : Person
axiom AttendsRegularly : Person → Prop
axiom StudiesDiligently : Person → Prop
axiom ExcelsInCourse : Person → Prop
axiom Rule : ∀ p : Person, AttendsRegularly p → StudiesDiligently p → ExcelsInCourse p
axiom Diligent : StudiesDiligently Priya
axiom NotExcel : ¬ ExcelsInCourse Priya

theorem not_attended : ¬ AttendsRegularly Priya :=
  fun (hAttend : AttendsRegularly Priya) =>
    have mustExcel : ExcelsInCourse Priya := Rule Priya hAttend Diligent
    NotExcel mustExcel
</lean>
