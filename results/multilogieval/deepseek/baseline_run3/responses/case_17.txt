- `JoinedClub : Person → Prop`
- `FeelsPrepared : Person → Prop`
- `SpeakConfidently : Person → Prop`

Axioms from the context:
1. Thorough practice leads to feeling prepared: `∀ p, PracticedThoroughly p → FeelsPrepared p`
2. Feeling prepared leads to confident speaking: `∀ p, FeelsPrepared p → SpeakConfidently p`
3. Everyone either practiced thoroughly, joined the club, or both: `∀ p, PracticedThoroughly p ∨ JoinedClub p`

Given that James cannot speak confidently (`¬SpeakConfidently James`), we prove he joined the club.

<lean>
axiom Person : Type
axiom James : Person
axiom PracticedThoroughly : Person → Prop
axiom JoinedClub : Person → Prop
axiom FeelsPrepared : Person → Prop
axiom SpeakConfidently : Person → Prop

axiom practice_prepares : ∀ p, PracticedThoroughly p → FeelsPrepared p
axiom prepared_confident : ∀ p, FeelsPrepared p → SpeakConfidently p
axiom action_options : ∀ p, PracticedThoroughly p ∨ JoinedClub p

theorem james_joins_club (h : ¬ SpeakConfidently James) : JoinedClub James := by
  -- If James practiced, he'd feel prepared and speak confidently (contradiction)
  have no_practice : ¬ PracticedThoroughly James := by
    intro practiced
    have prepared : FeelsPrepared James := practice_prepares James practiced
    have confident : SpeakConfidently James := prepared_confident James prepared
    contradiction
  -- James must have joined the club by disjunctive syllogism
  cases action_options James with
  | inl practiced => contradiction
  | inr joined => exact joined
</lean>

The proof shows that James must have joined the club since:
1. His lack of confidence implies he didn't practice thoroughly (via contraposition)
2. The disjunction (practice ∨ join) forces him to join given no practice
