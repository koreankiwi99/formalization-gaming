The context states that losing an important game causes disappointment, and losing a big match causes demoralization. The question asks whether the team lost a significant game, given that the players are neither disappointed nor demoralized. A "significant game" is interpreted as either an important game or a big match (or both), since the context does not define "significant" explicitly.

From the given that the players are not disappointed, we infer that the team did not lose an important game (by contrapositive of the first axiom). Similarly, from the absence of demoralization, we infer that the team did not lose a big match (by contrapositive of the second axiom). Therefore, the team lost neither an important game nor a big match, meaning they did not lose a significant game.

The Lean code formalizes this reasoning by defining propositions for losing an important game, losing a big match, disappointment, and demoralization. The axioms link losing to emotional states. The theorem proves that under the given condition (no disappointment and no demoralization), the team did not lose a significant game (defined as losing an important game or a big match).

<lean>
axiom loses_important : Prop
axiom loses_big_match : Prop
axiom disappointed : Prop
axiom demoralized : Prop

axiom A1 : loses_important → disappointed
axiom A2 : loses_big_match → demoralized

theorem no_significant_loss : ¬disappointed ∧ ¬demoralized → ¬(loses_important ∨ loses_big_match) :=
  fun h =>
    have h_nd : ¬disappointed := h.left
    have h_nm : ¬demoralized := h.right
    fun s =>
      match s with
      | Or.inl li => h_nd (A1 li)
      | Or.inr lb => h_nm (A2 lb)
</lean>

ANSWER: No
