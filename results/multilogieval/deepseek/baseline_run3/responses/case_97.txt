The context provides several implications and a disjunction about events at school. The goal is to determine if the conclusion "if kids are not hyper all afternoon, then the students had lots of homework" follows from the given statements.

### Logical Representation:
- **S**: Substitute teacher is present.
- **P**: Pizza is served for lunch.
- **G**: Students goof off in class.
- **H**: Kids are hyper all afternoon.
- **L**: Students learn much that day.
- **HW**: Students have lots of homework.

### Axioms:
1. If there's a substitute teacher, students goof off: `S → G`.
2. If pizza is served, kids are hyper: `P → H`.
3. Either substitute teacher or pizza (or both): `S ∨ P`.
4. If students goof off, they don't learn much: `G → ¬L`.
5. If they don't learn much, they have lots of homework: `¬L → HW`.

### Theorem to Prove:
The question asks: "If kids will not be hyper all afternoon, did the students have lots of homework?" This translates to proving `¬H → HW`.

### Proof Strategy:
1. Assume `¬H` (kids are not hyper).
2. From `P → H` (axiom 2) and `¬H`, derive `¬P` (by contraposition).
3. From `S ∨ P` (axiom 3) and `¬P`, derive `S` (disjunctive syllogism).
4. From `S` and `S → G` (axiom 1), derive `G`.
5. From `G` and `G → ¬L` (axiom 4), derive `¬L`.
6. From `¬L` and `¬L → HW` (axiom 5), derive `HW`.

Thus, `¬H → HW` is provable from the axioms.

### Lean Code:
<lean>
axiom S : Prop
axiom P : Prop
axiom G : Prop
axiom H : Prop
axiom L : Prop
axiom HW : Prop

axiom A1 : S → G
axiom A2 : P → H
axiom A3 : S ∨ P
axiom A4 : G → ¬L
axiom A5 : ¬L → HW

theorem main : ¬H → HW := by
  intro h_not_hyper  -- Assume ¬H
  have h_not_pizza : ¬P := by
    intro h_pizza
    apply h_not_hyper
    exact A2 h_pizza  -- P → H, so ¬H → ¬P
  cases A3 with
  | inl h_sub =>
    have h_goof : G := A1 h_sub      -- S → G
    have h_no_learn : ¬L := A4 h_goof -- G → ¬L
    exact A5 h_no_learn              -- ¬L → HW
  | inr h_pizza =>
    contradiction  -- Contradiction: ¬P and P
</lean>

The Lean proof confirms `¬H → HW` using the axioms. The `cases` tactic handles the disjunction `S ∨ P`. In the `inl` branch (substitute teacher present), the chain of implications leads to `HW`. In the `inr` branch (pizza served), `¬P` (derived from `¬H`) contradicts `P`, proving `HW` by contradiction.

ANSWER: Yes
