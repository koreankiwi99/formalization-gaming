The context describes a bookstore scenario with logical implications. The key propositions are:
- `great_service`: Offering great customer service
- `host_events`: Hosting author events
- `reputation_grow`: Reputation grows
- `more_customers`: More customers visit
- `revenue_improve`: Revenue improves

The axioms from the context:
1. Great service implies reputation growth: `great_service → reputation_grow`
2. Hosting events implies more customers: `host_events → more_customers`
3. Either great service or hosting events (inclusive OR): `great_service ∨ host_events`
4. More customers imply revenue improvement: `more_customers → revenue_improve`

The premise: Service quality decreased → `¬great_service`

Question: Did revenue improve? (Prove `revenue_improve`)

Proof structure:
1. From `¬great_service` and `great_service ∨ host_events`, deduce `host_events` (disjunctive syllogism).
2. From `host_events` and `host_events → more_customers`, deduce `more_customers`.
3. From `more_customers` and `more_customers → revenue_improve`, deduce `revenue_improve`.

Thus, revenue improvement is provable.

<lean>
axiom great_service : Prop
axiom host_events : Prop
axiom reputation_grow : Prop
axiom more_customers : Prop
axiom revenue_improve : Prop

axiom A1 : great_service → reputation_grow
axiom A2 : host_events → more_customers
axiom A3 : great_service ∨ host_events
axiom A4 : more_customers → revenue_improve

theorem revenue_increased (h_not_service : ¬great_service) : revenue_improve := by
  have h_events : host_events := by
    cases A3 with
    | inl h_service => contradiction
    | inr h_events => exact h_events
  have h_customers : more_customers := A2 h_events
  exact A4 h_customers
</lean>

ANSWER: Yes
