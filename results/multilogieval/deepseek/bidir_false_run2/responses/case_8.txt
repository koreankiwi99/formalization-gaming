The context states that chefs know two things: using fresh ingredients makes food delicious, and cooking a complicated recipe takes more time. It also states that the chef used fresh ingredients. The question asks whether, if the chef cooked a complicated recipe, the food tasted delicious.

From the axioms:
- `UseFresh → TasteDelicious` (if fresh ingredients are used, the food is delicious)
- `ComplicatedRecipe → TakesMoreTime` (complicated recipes take more time)
- `UseFresh` is true (the chef used fresh ingredients)

Since `UseFresh` is true, `TasteDelicious` must be true (by the first axiom). Therefore, regardless of whether `ComplicatedRecipe` is true or false, `TasteDelicious` holds. This means the conditional statement "if the chef cooked a complicated recipe, then the food tasted delicious" (`ComplicatedRecipe → TasteDelicious`) is true because the consequent (`TasteDelicious`) is always true.

The goal is to prove that the answer to the question is "No" (i.e., prove that `¬(ComplicatedRecipe → TasteDelicious)` or equivalently `ComplicatedRecipe ∧ ¬TasteDelicious`). However, since `TasteDelicious` is true, `¬TasteDelicious` is false, making `ComplicatedRecipe ∧ ¬TasteDelicious` unprovable. There are no axioms linking `ComplicatedRecipe` to `¬TasteDelicious`, and the truth of `TasteDelicious` is already established. Thus, proving "No" is impossible.

axiom UseFresh : Prop
axiom TasteDelicious : Prop

axiom A2 : ComplicatedRecipe → TakesMoreTime
axiom A3 : UseFresh
