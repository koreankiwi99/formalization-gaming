The conclusion we are testing is: "James watches cooking shows for inspiration" (denoted as `W James`). We are required to prove the negation of this conclusion (i.e., `¬W James`) given the premises and the statement that James does not gain confidence in the kitchen (`¬G James`).

### Step 1: Define Predicates and Axioms
- **Entities**: James is an individual (axiom `James : obj`).
- **Predicates**:
  - `F x`: Chef `x` follows recipes precisely.
  - `W x`: Chef `x` watches cooking shows for inspiration.
  - `D x`: Dishes of chef `x` turn out well.
  - `G x`: Chef `x` gains confidence in the kitchen.
- **Premises**:
  1. If a chef follows recipes precisely, their dishes turn out well: `∀ x, F x → D x`.
  2. If dishes turn out well, the chef gains confidence: `∀ x, D x → G x`.
  3. A chef either follows recipes precisely, watches cooking shows, or does both: `∀ x, F x ∨ W x`.
- **Additional Given for James**: James does not gain confidence: `¬G James`.

### Step 2: Attempt to Prove the Negation
Our goal is to prove `¬W James` (James does not watch cooking shows). We use Lean to formalize the reasoning:
- From premises 1 and 2, we derive `F James → G James` (transitivity).
- Since `¬G James` (given), we use modus tollens to get `¬F James`.
- From premise 3 (`F James ∨ W James`), we know James must have either `F` or `W`.
- With `¬F James`, the disjunction simplifies to `W James`.

However, this means we **cannot** prove `¬W James` because the premises actually require `W James` to hold. Attempting to construct a proof of `¬W James` leads to a dead end, as the case `W James` is consistent with the premises and does not yield a contradiction.

axiom W : obj → Prop -- watches cooking shows
axiom D : obj → Prop -- dishes turn out well
axiom G : obj → Prop -- gains confidence
-- Premises

axiom P2 : ∀ (x : obj), D x → G x -- dishes well → gains confidence
axiom P3 : ∀ (x : obj), F x ∨ W x -- either follows recipes or watches shows (or both)
axiom NotGJames : ¬G James -- James lacks confidence
-- Attempt to prove ¬W James
theorem not_W_James : ¬W James := by
  -- Prove F James → G James (using P1 and P2)
  have F_imp_G : F James → G James := fun hF => P2 James (P1 James hF)

  have not_F : ¬F James := mt F_imp_G NotGJames

      -- No contradiction arises, so the proof fails
