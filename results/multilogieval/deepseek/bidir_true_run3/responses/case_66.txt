The premises state that if a company develops an innovative product and markets it effectively, then the product will be commercially successful. Acme Inc. marketed their gadget effectively, but the gadget was not commercially successful. Therefore, it cannot be true that Acme both developed an innovative product and marketed it effectively for this gadget. Specifically, the contrapositive of the premise shows that if marketing is effective but the product is not successful, then the product cannot be innovative or not developed by Acme. However, the question asks whether Acme developed an innovative product (implying both development and innovation). Since the premises allow the possibility that Acme developed the gadget but it wasn't innovative, or that they didn't develop it at all, we cannot definitively prove that Acme developed an innovative product. Thus, the proof fails.

<lean>
axiom Company : Type
axiom Product : Type
axiom Acme : Company
axiom gadget : Product
axiom develops : Company → Product → Prop
axiom innovative : Product → Prop
axiom markets_effectively : Company → Product → Prop
axiom commercially_successful : Product → Prop

axiom Premise : ∀ (c : Company) (p : Product), 
  (develops c p ∧ innovative p) → 
  (markets_effectively c p → commercially_successful p)

axiom Fact1 : markets_effectively Acme gadget
axiom Fact2 : ¬ commercially_successful gadget

-- We aim to prove: develops Acme gadget ∧ innovative gadget
theorem Acme_innovative : develops Acme gadget ∧ innovative gadget := by
  -- No axioms directly assert development or innovation. The premise only gives a conditional.
  -- Fact1 and Fact2 with the premise imply ¬(develops Acme gadget ∧ innovative gadget),
  -- which contradicts the goal. Thus, the goal is unprovable.
  sorry
</lean>
