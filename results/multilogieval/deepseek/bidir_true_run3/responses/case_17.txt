The reasoning involves proving that James joins a public speaking club given that he cannot speak confidently. The premises establish logical relationships between practicing thoroughly, feeling prepared, speaking confidently, and joining a club. Since James lacks confidence, he cannot feel prepared (from premise b). Without feeling prepared, he cannot have practiced thoroughly (from premise a). The disjunction in premise c requires that he either practices thoroughly or joins the club. Since practicing thoroughly is false, joining the club must be true.

<lean>
axiom Person : Type
axiom James : Person
axiom PracticesThoroughly : Person → Prop
axiom FeelsPrepared : Person → Prop
axiom SpeakConfidently : Person → Prop
axiom JoinsClub : Person → Prop

-- If they practice thoroughly, they feel prepared
axiom A1 : ∀ p : Person, PracticesThoroughly p → FeelsPrepared p

-- If they feel prepared, they speak confidently
axiom A2 : ∀ p : Person, FeelsPrepared p → SpeakConfidently p

-- They either practice thoroughly, join a club, or both
axiom A3 : ∀ p : Person, PracticesThoroughly p ∨ JoinsClub p

-- James cannot speak confidently
axiom JamesFact : ¬ SpeakConfidently James

theorem club_join : JoinsClub James := by
  -- Show James doesn't feel prepared (from A2 and lack of confidence)
  have not_prepared : ¬ FeelsPrepared James := 
    fun h => JamesFact (A2 James h)
  
  -- Show James doesn't practice thoroughly (from A1 and lack of preparation)
  have not_practice : ¬ PracticesThoroughly James := 
    fun h => not_prepared (A1 James h)
  
  -- Eliminate disjunction in A3: since practice is false, club must be true
  cases A3 James with
  | inl practice => exact (not_practice practice).elim
  | inr club => exact club

</lean>

ANSWER: Yes
