The context states that for athletes, training with dedication and getting adequate rest implies peak performance. Simone got adequate rest but did not perform at her peak. We need to determine if Simone trained with dedication.

In logical terms:
- Let `T a` mean "athlete a trains with dedication".
- Let `R a` mean "athlete a gets adequate rest".
- Let `P a` mean "athlete a performs at peak".
- Premise: ∀a, T a → (R a → P a)
- Given: R Simone and ¬P Simone

We wish to prove: T Simone

However, from the premises:
1. Assume T Simone (for contradiction).
2. Since R Simone, by the premise we get P Simone.
3. But ¬P Simone, which contradicts P Simone.

Thus, the assumption T Simone must be false. We can prove ¬T Simone, but we cannot prove T Simone. Therefore, the affirmative statement "Simone trained with dedication" cannot be proven from the premises.

<lean>
axiom Athlete : Type
axiom Simone : Athlete
axiom TrainsWithDedication : Athlete → Prop
axiom GetsAdequateRest : Athlete → Prop
axiom PerformAtPeak : Athlete → Prop

axiom context : ∀ a : Athlete, TrainsWithDedication a → (GetsAdequateRest a → PerformAtPeak a)
axiom givenRest : GetsAdequateRest Simone
axiom givenNotPeak : ¬ PerformAtPeak Simone

-- Attempt to prove TrainsWithDedication Simone
theorem prove_dedication : TrainsWithDedication Simone := by
  -- We cannot prove this. Instead, we can prove the opposite:
  have contra : TrainsWithDedication Simone → PerformAtPeak Simone := by
    intro h
    exact context Simone h givenRest
  -- This leads to contradiction with givenNotPeak, so TrainsWithDedication Simone is false.
  -- But we cannot prove the positive claim.
  sorry -- Proof fails

-- Since we cannot complete the proof, we conclude Failure.
</lean>

ANSWER: Failure
