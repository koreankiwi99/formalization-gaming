The context states that if a company develops an innovative product and markets it effectively, then the product will be commercially successful. For Acme Inc., we know they marketed their gadget effectively, but it was not commercially successful. Therefore, Acme's gadget cannot be innovative, as per the logical implication.

<lean>
axiom Company : Type
axiom Product : Type
axiom Acme : Company
axiom gadget : Product
axiom Develops : Company → Product → Prop
axiom Innovative : Product → Prop
axiom MarketedEffectively : Company → Product → Prop
axiom CommerciallySuccessful : Product → Prop

axiom marketed_effectively : MarketedEffectively Acme gadget
axiom not_successful : ¬ CommerciallySuccessful gadget
axiom developed : Develops Acme gadget
axiom business_rule : ∀ (c : Company) (p : Product), 
  (Develops c p ∧ Innovative p) → (MarketedEffectively c p → CommerciallySuccessful p)

theorem not_innovative : ¬ Innovative gadget := by
  intro hInnovative
  have H : Develops Acme gadget ∧ Innovative gadget := ⟨developed, hInnovative⟩
  have H2 : MarketedEffectively Acme gadget → CommerciallySuccessful gadget := business_rule Acme gadget H
  have H3 : CommerciallySuccessful gadget := H2 marketed_effectively
  exact not_successful H3
</lean>

The Lean proof shows that the gadget cannot be innovative. Therefore, Acme Inc. did not develop an innovative product (specifically, the gadget is not innovative).

ANSWER: No
