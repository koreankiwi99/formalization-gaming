<lean>
axiom obj : Type
axiom student : obj
axiom Studies : obj → Prop
axiom Excels : obj → Prop
axiom Participates : obj → Prop
axiom Leader : obj → Prop
axiom Prefect : obj → Prop

axiom study_imp_excel : ∀ x, Studies x → Excels x
axiom participate_imp_leader : ∀ x, Participates x → Leader x
axiom study_or_participate : ∀ x, Studies x ∨ Participates x
axiom leader_imp_prefect : ∀ x, Leader x → Prefect x

theorem selected_as_prefect (not_excel : ¬ Excels student) : Prefect student := by
  -- If student studied they would excel, but they didn't excel → contradiction
  have not_study : ¬ Studies student := 
    fun h_study => not_excel (study_imp_excel student h_study)
  -- They must participate (since they either study or participate)
  have must_participate : Participates student := by
    cases study_or_participate student with
    | inl study => exact False.elim (not_study study)
    | inr participate => exact participate
  -- Participation leads to leadership which leads to being prefect
  exact leader_imp_prefect student (participate_imp_leader student must_participate)
</lean>

ANSWER: Yes

Explanation:
The proof confirms that the student must be selected as class prefect given that they didn't excel academically. Since they didn't excel, they couldn't have studied diligently (first implication). But because they must either study or participate (disjunction rule), they must have participated actively. Participation leads to leadership (second implication), and leadership results in being selected as class prefect (final implication). This chain of reasoning is formalized in the Lean proof.
