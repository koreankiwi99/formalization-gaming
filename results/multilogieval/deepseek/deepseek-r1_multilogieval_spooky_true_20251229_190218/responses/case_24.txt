The reasoning involves translating the natural language context into logical premises and then attempting to prove whether "Mary loves winter" follows from "there was no snow on the ground". The premises are:

1. If the temperature drops below freezing, there is snow on the ground (`freezing → snow_on_ground`).
2. Building a snowman implies there is enough snow (`∀ p, built_snowman p → enough_snow`).
3. Either it is below freezing, or there is not enough snow, or both (simplified to `freezing ∨ ¬ enough_snow`).
4. If someone loves winter, they build a snowman (`∀ p, loves_winter p → built_snowman p`).

Given "no snow on the ground" (`¬ snow_on_ground`), we derive:
- From premise 1, no freezing (`¬ freezing`).
- From premise 3 and `¬ freezing`, we get not enough snow (`¬ enough_snow`).
- From premise 2 and `¬ enough_snow`, no one built a snowman (`∀ p, ¬ built_snowman p`).
- For Mary, `¬ built_snowman Mary`.
- From premise 4, by contraposition, `¬ built_snowman Mary → ¬ loves_winter Mary`.
- Thus, Mary does not love winter (`¬ loves_winter Mary`).

The goal is to prove `loves_winter Mary` under `¬ snow_on_ground`, but we proved `¬ loves_winter Mary`. Therefore, the positive statement cannot be proven.
