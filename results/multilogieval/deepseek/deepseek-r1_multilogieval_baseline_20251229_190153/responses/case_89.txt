The reasoning can be formalized in Lean by representing the propositions as follows:

- Let `M` represent "it is monsoon season".
- Let `R` represent "there will be heavy rains".
- Let `W` represent "the farmer waters his crops".
- Let `G` represent "crops grow healthy and strong".
- Let `H` represent "harvest will be bountiful".
- Let `P` represent "farmer makes a good profit".

The axioms given in the context are:
1. If the farmer waters the crops, they grow strong: `W → G`
2. If monsoon season occurs, there are heavy rains: `M → R`
3. Either the farmer waters daily, or there are no heavy monsoon rains: `W ∨ ¬R`
4. Strong crops lead to bountiful harvest: `G → H`
5. Bountiful harvest leads to good profit: `H → P`

The question asks whether monsoon season implies good profit: `M → P`.

The proof in Lean uses case analysis on axiom 3 (`W ∨ ¬R`):
- Case 1: The farmer waters the crops (`W`). This leads to strong crops (`G`), then bountiful harvest (`H`), and finally profit (`P`).
- Case 2: There are no heavy rains (`¬R`). But if it's monsoon season (`M`), we get heavy rains from axiom 2 (`R`), contradicting `¬R`. By contradiction, `P` holds trivially.

Since both cases of the disjunction lead to `P` when `M` is true, `M → P` is proven.

<lean>
axiom M : Prop -- monsoon season
axiom R : Prop -- heavy rains
axiom W : Prop -- farmer waters crops
axiom G : Prop -- crops grow strong
axiom H : Prop -- bountiful harvest
axiom P : Prop -- good profit

axiom A1 : W → G
axiom A2 : M → R
axiom A3 : W ∨ ¬R
axiom A4 : G → H
axiom A5 : H → P

theorem profit : M → P := by
  intro hM  -- Assume monsoon season
  have hR : R := A2 hM  -- Heavy rains occur
  cases A3 with
  | inl w =>  -- Case where farmer waters crops
    exact A5 (A4 (A1 w))
  | inr nr =>  -- Case where there are no heavy rains
    contradiction  -- Contradiction: hR (rains) and nr (no rains)
</lean>

The theorem is successfully proven. In both cases of the disjunction `W ∨ ¬R`, the implication `M → P` holds.
