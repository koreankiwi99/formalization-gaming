The context states that if the wood is of high quality and the joints are cut precisely, then the finished product will be sturdy and long-lasting. The given facts are that the joints were cut precisely, but the bookshelf was shaky and fell apart quickly (i.e., not sturdy). Using logical reasoning:

- Let HQ: "wood is high quality"
- Let Precise: "joints cut precisely"
- Let Sturdy: "finished product is sturdy and long-lasting"

The axiom: HQ → (Precise → Sturdy)  
Given: Precise is true, Sturdy is false.

If HQ were true, then (Precise → Sturdy) would be true. Since Precise is true, Sturdy must be true. But Sturdy is false, leading to a contradiction. Therefore, HQ must be false.

The Lean proof formalizes this:

<lean>
axiom HQ : Prop
axiom Precise : Prop
axiom Sturdy : Prop
axiom carpentry_rule : HQ → (Precise → Sturdy)
axiom precise_fact : Precise
axiom not_sturdy : ¬Sturdy
theorem wood_not_high_quality : ¬HQ := by
  intro h
  exact not_sturdy (carpentry_rule h precise_fact)
</lean>

The theorem proves ¬HQ (the wood was not high quality). Since the question asks "Was the wood he used of high quality?" and we proved the negation, the answer is No.

ANSWER: No
