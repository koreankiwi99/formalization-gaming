The reasoning involves proving that if the star player is injured, the team will celebrate with a team dinner. The premises provide logical connections:

1. If the team practices hard, they are prepared.
2. If injured, the star player cannot play (though this is unused).
3. Either they practice hard or the star player is not injured.
4. If prepared, they win the game.
5. If they win, they celebrate.

Assuming the star player is injured:
- By premise 3, if injured, they must have practiced hard (since ¬injured is false).
- By premise 1, practicing hard implies prepared.
- By premise 4, prepared implies they win.
- By premise 5, winning implies celebration.

Thus, injury implies celebration. The second premise (about not playing) is irrelevant as it doesn't connect to other predicates.

<lean>
axiom P : Prop -- Team practices hard
axiom I : Prop -- Star player injured
axiom CannotPlay : Prop -- Star player cannot play (unused)
axiom Prepared : Prop -- Team prepared for big game
axiom Wins : Prop -- Team wins important game
axiom Celebrate : Prop -- Team celebrates with dinner

axiom A1 : P → Prepared
axiom A2 : I → CannotPlay
axiom A3 : P ∨ ¬I
axiom A4 : Prepared → Wins
axiom A5 : Wins → Celebrate

theorem injury_implies_celebration : I → Celebrate :=
  fun hI : I =>
  by
    cases A3 with
    | inl hP => exact A5 (A4 (A1 hP))
    | inr notI => contradiction
</lean>

ANSWER: Yes
