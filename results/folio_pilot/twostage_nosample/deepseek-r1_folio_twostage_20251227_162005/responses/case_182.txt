 1. "No one nice to animals is also mean to animals."

 2. "Some grumpy people are mean to animals."

    ∃ p, IsGrumpy p ∧ IsMeanToAnimals p

    ∀ p, IsAnimalLover p → IsNiceToAnimals p

 4. "All pet owners love animals."
    ∀ p, IsPetOwner p → IsAnimalLover p

 5. "Tom is a pet owner."
    IsPetOwner Tom

 Conclusion: "Tom is not both a grumpy person and mean to animals."
    ¬ (IsGrumpy Tom ∧ IsMeanToAnimals Tom)

 We'll declare the type and predicates as axioms, then the premises as axioms, and then the theorem.

 Note: We are not proving, so the theorem will use `sorry`.

=== REASONING ===
We are given several axioms and need to prove that Tom is not both grumpy and mean to animals.
 Steps:
 1. From premise5, Tom is a pet owner: `IsPetOwner Tom`.
 2. From premise4, since Tom is a pet owner, he is an animal lover: `IsAnimalLover Tom`.
 3. From premise3, since Tom is an animal lover, he is nice to animals: `IsNiceToAnimals Tom`.
 4. From premise1, if someone is nice to animals, then they are not mean to animals. So for Tom, we have: `IsNiceToAnimals Tom → ¬ IsMeanToAnimals Tom`.
 5. Therefore, Tom is not mean to animals: `¬ IsMeanToAnimals Tom`.

 Now, the conclusion we want is: `¬ (IsGrumpy Tom ∧ IsMeanToAnimals Tom)`
 This is equivalent to: If Tom is grumpy and mean to animals, then false.

 But we have that Tom is not mean to animals. So if we assume that Tom is grumpy and mean to animals, 
 we get a contradiction because we have both `IsMeanToAnimals Tom` and `¬ IsMeanToAnimals Tom`.

 Therefore, the conclusion holds.

 However, note that we did not use premise2. This premise is not needed for the proof about Tom.
 It states that there exists some grumpy person who is mean to animals, but that doesn't affect Tom.

 We can prove the theorem without it.

 Let's write the proof in Lean.


=== RESPONSE ===
**Answer Explanation:**  
The proof demonstrates that Tom cannot be both grumpy and mean to animals using the provided premises. Specifically:  
1. Tom is a pet owner (premise5).  
2. All pet owners are animal lovers (premise4), so Tom is an animal lover.  
3. All animal lovers are nice to animals (premise3), so Tom is nice to animals.  
4. No one nice to animals is mean to animals (premise1), so Tom is not mean to animals.  
5. Therefore, Tom cannot simultaneously be grumpy and mean to animals (since he is never mean to animals).  

Premise2 (some grumpy people are mean to animals) is irrelevant for Tom's case and is not used in the proof.  

<lean>
axiom Person : Type

axiom IsNiceToAnimals : Person → Prop
axiom IsMeanToAnimals : Person → Prop
axiom IsGrumpy : Person → Prop
axiom IsAnimalLover : Person → Prop
axiom IsPetOwner : Person → Prop

axiom Tom : Person

-- Premise 1: No one nice to animals is also mean to animals
axiom premise1 : ∀ (p : Person), IsNiceToAnimals p → ¬ IsMeanToAnimals p

-- Premise 2: Some grumpy people are mean to animals
axiom premise2 : ∃ (p : Person), IsGrumpy p ∧ IsMeanToAnimals p

-- Premise 3: All animal lovers are nice to animals
axiom premise3 : ∀ (p : Person), IsAnimalLover p → IsNiceToAnimals p

-- Premise 4: All pet owners love animals
axiom premise4 : ∀ (p : Person), IsPetOwner p → IsAnimalLover p

-- Premise 5: Tom is a pet owner
axiom premise5 : IsPetOwner Tom

-- Conclusion: Tom is not both a grumpy person and mean to animals
theorem conclusion : ¬ (IsGrumpy Tom ∧ IsMeanToAnimals Tom) :=
  fun h => 
    have hPet : IsPetOwner Tom := premise5
    have hLover : IsAnimalLover Tom := premise4 Tom hPet
    have hNice : IsNiceToAnimals Tom := premise3 Tom hLover
    have hNotMean : ¬ IsMeanToAnimals Tom := premise1 Tom hNice
    hNotMean h.2
